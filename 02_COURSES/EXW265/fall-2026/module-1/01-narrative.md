    <!-- THE CONTEXT -->
    <section id="clinical-context">
        <h3 style="color: #000080; border-left: 5px solid #000080; padding-left: 15px;">The Prescription Gap</h3>
        <p>
            When working with special populations, exercise is not just a "workout"—it is a clinical intervention. The
            first <strong>Pattern</strong> we must audit is the medical diagnosis. How does a condition like Type 2
            Diabetes alter the body's response to movement?
        </p>
        <p>
            As a practitioner, your mission is to find the "Sweet Spot" of intensity. Too little movement results in no
            metabolic change; too much can lead to hypoglycemia or injury. This week, we examine how chronic conditions
            change the <strong>Rules</strong> of prescription.
        </p>
    </section>

    <hr style="border: 0; border-top: 1px solid #d4d0b8; margin: 30px 0;" />

    <!-- THE AUTHORITY -->
    <section id="ssot-grounding">
        <h3 style="color: #000080;">Grounding in the Authority</h3>
        <p>
            Our safety audits are strictly anchored in the <strong>SSoT (PAGA 2018)</strong>. We focus on Chapter 6,
            which provides federal guidelines for adults with chronic health conditions.
        </p>
        <div style="background-color: #eef4f8; padding: 20px; border-left: 5px solid #000080; margin: 20px 0;">
            <p style="margin: 0; font-style: italic;">
                "Physical activity is important for people with chronic medical conditions... it can improve their
                quality of life and reduce the risk of developing new conditions." (PAGA 2018, Ch. 6).
            </p>
