## Physical Activity Guidelines for Americans (PAGA 2018)
## Core Guidelines for EXW245: Exercise Testing & Prescription

### Exercise Intensity and Testing

The intensity of aerobic activity can be tracked in two ways—absolute intensity and relative intensity.

* **Absolute Intensity**: The amount of energy expended during the activity, commonly expressed as Metabolic Equivalents (METs). 1 MET is the rate of energy expenditure while sitting at rest.
    * **Light-intensity**: Less than 3.0 METs (e.g., walking at 2 mph or less).
    * **Moderate-intensity**: 3.0 to 5.9 METs (e.g., walking briskly at 2.5-4 mph).
    * **Vigorous-intensity**: 6.0 or more METs (e.g., jogging, running, carrying heavy loads).

* **Relative Intensity**: The level of effort required to do an activity relative to an individual's cardiorespiratory fitness. 
    * **Rating of Perceived Exertion (RPE)**: Estimated using a scale of 0 to 10 (0 is sitting, 10 is greatest effort possible). Moderate-intensity is a 5 or 6. Vigorous-intensity begins at a 7 or 8.
    * **The Talk Test**: A person doing moderate-intensity aerobic activity can talk, but not sing, during the activity. A person doing vigorous-intensity activity cannot say more than a few words without pausing for a breath.

### Exercise Prescription and Progression

Scientific studies indicate that the risk of injury is directly related to the gap between a person's usual level of activity and a new level of activity. 

* **Overload**: The size of the gap. Creating a small overload and waiting for the body to adapt and recover reduces the risk of injury.
* **Progression Rate**: Older adults appear to require more time to adapt to a new level of activity (in the range of 2 to 4 weeks) compared to youth and young adults. Less fit adults are at higher risk of injury when doing a given amount of activity compared to more fit adults. 

### Clinical Safety Rules

### Inactive people should "start low and go slow" by starting with lower intensity activities and gradually increasing how often and how long activities are done. ###

### When amounts of physical activity need to be increased to meet the key guidelines or personal goals, physical activity should be increased gradually over time, no matter what the person’s current level of physical activity. ###

### Avoid relatively vigorous-intensity activity, such as shoveling heavy snow or running. Adults with low fitness may need to start with light activity, or a mix of light- to moderate-intensity activity. ###

### Pay attention to the relative size of the increase in physical activity each week, as this is related to injury risk. ###

### If an overuse injury occurred in the past with a certain rate of progression, a person should increase activity more slowly the next time. ###

### Warming up before and cooling down after exercise are commonly recommended to prevent injuries and adverse cardiac events. ###

### Taking appropriate precautions means using the right gear and equipment, choosing safe environments in which to be active, following rules and policies, and making sensible choices about how, when, and where to be active. ###

### Be under the care of a health care provider if they have chronic conditions or symptoms. People with chronic conditions and symptoms can consult a health care professional or physical activity specialist about the types and amounts of activity appropriate for them. ###

## Clinical Examples (Mission Loop Framework)

### Clinical Example 1: Chris (Multiple Sclerosis)
**Pattern**: 53-year-old male with multiple sclerosis mapping out a goal to achieve 120 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week (30 minutes across 4 days).
**Rule**: PAGA Guidelines dictate using relative intensity to track exertion (maintaining a 5 or 6 on a 0-10 RPE scale) and applying the "start low and go slow" principle for progression.
**Solve**: Chris begins on a stationary bike for 20 minutes (RPE 5-6) just 2 days a week to ensure safe adaptation. Over two months, he successfully achieves overload and adapts, increasing to 30 minutes on 3 days a week. He integrates a 30-minute water exercise class, ultimately surpassing his goal to hit 160 minutes total per week.

### Clinical Example 2: Raymond (Type 2 Diabetes)
**Pattern**: 42-year-old male with Type 2 Diabetes who is seeking to establish an active baseline after receiving a step counter to track daily activity.
**Rule**: PAGA standard recommends at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week, augmented by muscle-strengthening activities on 2 or more days.
**Solve**: Raymond successfully maps his activity to manageable daily micro-sessions: walking briskly 10 minutes daily to the bus, a 25-minute lunch walk 3 times a week, and a 25-minute weekend bike ride. To meet muscle-strengthening requirements, he completes body-weight exercises (push-ups, lunges, planks, squats) 3 nights a week while watching TV.
