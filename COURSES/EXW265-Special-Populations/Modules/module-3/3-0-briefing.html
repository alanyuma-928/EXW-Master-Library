Key Concept: The P-T-C-F Framework

To ensure high-fidelity clinical processing, all prompts must follow this structure:

  1. Persona: Give the AI a specific expert role (e.g., "Clinical Exercise Physiologist").
  2. Task: State exactly what needs to be done (e.g., "Audit this metabolic circuit").
  3. Context: Provide localized constraints (e.g., "Yuma heat protocols, PAGA 2018 standards").
  4. Format: Define the output structure (e.g., "LaTeX table with MET values").

Part 2 (The Audit):
"The AI successfully utilized the Persona to provide a clinical calculation rather than a generic fitness tip. It calculated the metabolic cost at 3.5 METs for a brisk walk, which aligns with PAGA 2018 Chapter 4 standards for older adults. However, the AI initially suggested a heart-rate-based intensity guide. As Auditor, I retrofitted this to use the Borg RPE Scale (12-14) because the client's 'Context' included Stage 1 Hypertension medication, which can blunt heart rate response. The AI's second draft correctly incorporated this adjustment."

Part 3 (The Presence-Check):
"I argue that Persona assignment is the most critical P-T-C-F variable for high-risk populations. Without an expert persona, Large Language Models tend to default to 'General Wellness' advice, which is insufficient for clinical populations. By forcing the AI to 'act' as a Clinical Exercise Physiologist, I am triggering its internal weights for medical evidence over general web data. This expert boundary is essential in Yuma, where an automated suggestion for 'vigorous' activity at 105°F could lead to a syncopal episode. My persona 'Lock' ensures that the safety thresholds of the ACSM 12th Ed are prioritized over raw performance optimization."

⚠️ Safety Gate: A prompt is only as safe as its grounding. Always cite the **PAGA 2018** or **ACSM 12th Ed** within the 'Context' field of your prompt.
