📖 Instructional Content

In the **Architect Phase**, we utilize the **Hierarchy of Safety**. When two clinical guidelines conflict, the Resident must prioritize the system with the highest immediate risk (usually Cardiovascular/Glycemic over Structural).

Key Concept: The Clinical Collision

Common interference patterns in the Yuma/AWC Resident network include:

  • Osteoporosis vs. Hypertension: High-load lifting vs. intra-thoracic pressure risks.
  • Arthritis vs. Metabolic Syndrome: Joint-sparing needs vs. high-volume movement requirements.
  • Type 1 Diabetes vs. Heart Disease: Glycemic volatility vs. blunted heart rate response from meds.

🧪 Laboratory: The Architect Synthesis Profile

1. The P-T-C-F Prompt:

###
Persona: You are a Clinical AI Architect.
Task: Synthesize a safe exercise protocol for a 70-year-old with three conditions.
Context: Client has Severe Osteoporosis (T-score -3.0), Stage 2 Hypertension (165/105), and Osteoarthritis in the knees. AI suggested "Heavy deadlifts and jumping jacks for bone density."
Format: 3-Part Synthesis Report grounded in ACSM 12th Ed hierarchy of safety.
###

2. The Clinical Audit: Audit the AI output against the **ACSM 12th Edition**. Identify exactly how "jumping jacks" violates the arthritis redline and how "heavy deadlifts" violates the hypertension redline. Propose a "Synthesis Retrofit" that addresses bone density without triggering a cardiac or joint flare-up.

3. The Presence Defense: Submit a 150-word Verbatim Text Defense. In your own clinical voice, justify why you prioritized "Cardiac Safety" over "Bone Density" in this specific Yuma-based simulation.

⚠️ Safety Gate: Architect Redline: In a multi-morbidity synthesis, the "Weakest Link" (the most unstable system) dictates the intensity of the entire session. Never allow an AI to "average" clinical risks.
