<h2> SOP: The Delimiter Protocol (Data Firewalls)
Team, listen up. In clinical exercise science, precision is everything. If your instructions "bleed" into your client data, the AI "Engine" gets confused, leading to dangerous prescriptions. We solve this using the Delimiter Protocol—the structural firewall that separates the Pilot's orders from the Client's facts.

<h3> 🟥 The Rule: The Triple-Hash Standard (###)
In every prompt you engineer, you must wrap "Active Data" (case studies, client profiles, medical history) inside triple-hash delimiters.

The Symbol: ###

The Logic: This tells the AI: "Everything inside these symbols is the data you must analyze. Everything outside is the instruction on how to do it."

<h3> 🟨 The Pattern: Identifying "Active Data"
If the information can change from one client to the next, it is Active Data. This includes:

Blood Pressure/Glucose readings.

Medical history or chronic conditions.

Current activity levels or MET-minute totals.

PAGA PDF text or specific chapter excerpts.

<h3> 🟦 The Solve: The "Centaur" Prompt Structure
Use this structure for every lab. Notice how the clinical variables are "caged" to prevent them from corrupting the instruction set.

Correct Implementation:

[P] Persona: Senior Clinical Exercise Physiologist.
[T] Task: Audit this client for hypoglycemia risk.
[C] Context: Reference PAGA Chapter 8 safety limits.

### [ACTIVE DATA START] ###
Client: 55-year-old female, T2D, sedentary.
Pre-Ex Glucose: 95 mg/dL.
Blood Pressure: 145/92 mmHg.
### [ACTIVE DATA END] ###

<h3> 🏛️ The Architect's Verdict
Delimiters are the difference between a professional audit and a casual chat. By "caging" your data, you enable the AI to focus its maximum probabilistic power on the clinical facts without being distracted by your narrative.

"If it isn't wrapped in hashes, it isn't isolated. If it isn't isolated, it isn't clinical."
