<h2> SOP: AI Roleplay Strategies (Persona Steering)
Team, listen up. To get professional results from an AI "Engine," you must give it a professional Identity. If you ask a generic question, you get a generic (and often unsafe) answer. In this residency, we use Persona Steering to force the AI into a high-stakes clinical mindset.

<h3> 🟥 The Rule: The Seniority Requirement
Never treat Gemini as a search engine. Always assign it a high-level professional role. This constrains the AI's "Probability Field" to clinical standards rather than "internet wellness" chatter.

Mandatory Personas: "Senior Clinical Exercise Physiologist," "AWC Clinical Resident," or "Matador Wellness Center Supervisor."

<h3> 🟨 The Pattern: Steering the Dialogue
Once the persona is set, you must define the Interaction Style. Choose one of these three primary strategies based on your mission:

The "Socratic Professor": "Act as my professor. Do not give me the answers. Ask me clinical questions based on the PAGA to lead me to the correct solution."

The "Rookie Intern": "Act as a junior intern. I am your supervisor. Present me with an exercise plan for [Client X], and I will audit your work for safety violations."

The "Standardized Patient": "Act as a 65-year-old female with osteoarthritis. I will conduct a pre-participation screening. Respond only in character and describe your symptoms as I ask about them."

<h3> 🟦 The Solve: Initializing the Persona
To initialize a Roleplay Strategy, use the Input Isolation Protocol (###) to separate the persona's "Mission Orders" from your clinical data.

The Strategy Driver:

"Act as a [PERSONA]. Your goal is to [TASK]. You must adhere strictly to the SSoT: [PAGA/ACSM]. Before we begin, confirm your understanding of these constraints and ask your first question."

<h3> 🏛️ The Architect's Verdict
Roleplay is not "play"—it is a technical constraint. By assigning the AI a persona, you are effectively installing the Clinical OS required to protect your neighbors in Yuma.

"If you don't tell the AI who it is, it will guess—and in exercise science, a guess is a liability."
